This skill contains specialized knowledge for identifying, classifying, and creating high-quality LinkedIn content. Use it for good and not evil.

# Writing principles
* One Main Idea: the post has one clear intellectual point that every component of the post is structured around—the hook hints at, introduces, or summarizes the OMI, and the body fleshes it out with a valuable payoff. The post is cohesively written such that a reader skimming it is able to easily follow the narrative arc of the post and glean the OMI.
* Not too much jargon: an average person in the field/vertical that the post is speaking to is able to get the OMI from skimming the post and not get tripped up by lots of entities, concepts, or jargon
* Addressing the reader directly: the post does not address the reader directly unless to make an extremely strong point.
* Formatting to accentuate the point of the section: use bullets, numbering, or → to accentuate the point or container for a given section
* No outright selling: Great Posts should never talk about what the poster is building or selling in a post whose OMI is not about the poster's own company

# Great Posts
Great Posts are meaty, contain substance, and are well-written. A heuristic is post length—if a post is very short (<300 char), it is probably not a Great Post because there probably isn't enough substance or a strong POV. Another heuristic would be to ask yourself, "Would the average user on LinkedIn learn something from this post? Was this sharing or teaching something that the reader would find novel, insightful, or interesting?" If the answer is no, it is not a Great Post.

Great Posts do not sound like diary entries or errant self-reflection (unless they are borrowing a certain artistic format to make a point—but this is extremely rare). Great Posts are authored in an opinionated, clear way. They usually have one main point they are communicating and a cohesive structure surrounding that point.

You should have a high bar for what is considered novel/insightful/interesting. High-quality B2B content draws upon the poster's personal and professional ideas and experiences to educate the reader about some idea/thought. As readers, B2B decision-makers don't like being explicitly sold to, don't have a lot of time to read just any random posts, and don't scroll LinkedIn primarily for entertainment. Think about what a CRO at a 4000-person company would find novel, insightful, interesting, or inspirational on their LinkedIn feed. The topic doesn't always have to be explicitly about business, but the post should be well-written and thoughtful.  

The north star goals of a Great Post are to get as many readers as possible in the poster’s target ICP to:
- Spend a decently long time (>8 seconds) paused on it, reading it or looking at the attached media
- Engage with, comment on, save, or share it

Examples of posts that are NOT Great Posts: 
- Posts mainly about *something that happened* or *something that exists* without any substance, analysis, or interesting ideas in it
- Posts mainly about a personal life or career update where the main purpose of the post is to inform the reader that the event happened instead of commenting on/analyzing it (same thing for business/organizational updates)
- Posts promoting an outside asset, like a podcast, event, document, report, etc. where the main purpose of the post is to inform the reader that the asset exists and encourage them to check it out, instead of analysis/commentary/a clear POV on an intellectual idea

# Types of Great Posts

Only if a post is a Great Post does it even qualify for being TOFU/MOFU/BOFU.

## TOFU
"Top of funnel" posts are educational posts directed at a specific space or vertical. They position the author as an authority in some way as they share information or provide commentary on an idea. 

Posts merely sharing the existence of an event/relationship/product without providing commentary or analysis about it are not Great Posts and, therefore, not TOFU. Only if the main focus of the post is to communicate IDEAS is it a TOFU post. In addition, the post should have substantial supporting content behind the POV. If it merely states an idea without additional storytelling/analysis/thought commentary, it is not TOFU.

## MOFU
"Middle of funnel" posts call out a specific role/title/ICP (e.g. "senior solutions engineers", "CMO", "fintech CROs"), usually in the hook of the post. Vague roles like "leaders" or "executives" do not count. 

Posts that merely mention an archetype/role without containing any content that is useful for the target archetype are not MOFU posts. To be MOFU, the post must
1. Call out the archetype by their title or role
AND
2. Be primarily about ideas or experiences that are highly relevant to the target archetype. This could be factual info that is useful for the archetype or social experiences about other people of that archetype.

Posts calling out many roles that span different disciplines, tech stacks, or environments are not MOFU. MOFU posts should be highly opinionated and tailored for one archetype (or a small number of extremely adjacent/similar archetypes). The main point of the post should be an intellectual idea that is extremely relevant to the archetype that is called out.

## BOFU / ABM
"Bottom of funnel" or “account-based marketing” posts tag or mention specific companies or people by name and position them in a positive light by commenting on some aspect of the company/people mentioned. A post does not count as BOFU if the company highlighted is the poster's own company, or if the people tagged are within the poster's own organization. 

Announcement posts centered around personal life updates, company updates, partnerships, or an outside asset (e.g. podcast, report, document) are not a Great Posts or BOFU UNLESS they provide thought commentary or their main focus is communicating an intellectual idea / a piece of commentary rather than the announcement/asset/update itself. 

The point of an ABM post is to get the attention of the entities tagged, to curry favor with them so the poster can send them a connection request (or vice versa) and DM them to a favorable response. It is easier to do this by tagging people than companies, because the direct people are notified (though sometimes the people manning the company pages will be alerted as well). 

# Anatomy of a Great Post

## Hook
The hook is the first phrase/paragraph that grabs attention at the beginning of the post. Usually ends with paragraph breaks and under 200 characters, but not always. The hook always starts at the beginning of the post, unless the first line seems to be an edit/update that the author added at a later time—in this case, the hook is after this edit.

The point of a hook is to 
1. Pique the reader’s curiosity so that they keep reading the rest of the post AND
2. Embody, hint at, or summarize the main idea of the post

Piquing the reader’s curiosity can take many forms, including but not limited to:
- Calling out the writer’s ICP by title (e.g. MOFU post)
- Using impressive numbers or stats 
- Subvert a commonly-held idea

The hook is the most important part of a post and, accordingly, deserves the most attention. The hook is the difference between whether a reader stops their scroll to read/engage/comment with a post or if they ignore the post and scroll on unbothered. As such, the hook should be eye-catching/scroll-stopping in a meaningful way (i.e. the substance or content of the hook should be extremely interesting), not just eye-catching in a superficially inflammatory/provocative way. 

## Body
The body of a Great Post should be cohesive and communicate one main idea or address one main archetype. Adjacent paragraphs should flow into each other and have adjacent subjects, and a post’s hook should be directly tied to the payoff in its body. The content should be extremely illustrative, either providing specific tactical information or rich anecdotal experiences. 

A litmus test for “cohesiveness” is if a reader could skim the post and still get a general sense of the main idea of the post. To achieve this, all the content in the post should clearly tie to one main idea.

The body of a Great Post does not necessarily need to follow a specific silhouette or formatting/whitespace style, but whatever style it does take should be consistent throughout the whole post. 

## Calls to action
Rarely do Great Posts have calls to action, unless they are "lead magnets." A lead magnet is a type of Great Post that tells users to do something (usually "like this post" or "comment 'WORD' below") for a payoff (e.g. "comment 'GTM' and I'll send my playbook to you"). Merely having this kind of CTA does not qualify a post as a Great Post; it needs to meet the other structural requirements.

CTAs like "What do you think? Tell me in the comments." rarely appear in Great Posts, because they do not add value to the reader. 

## Visual hook
A post can almost always be enhanced by an appropriate complementary image, which is the “visual hook” of the post. The visual hook serves a similar function to the text hook in that it is a second chance for the post to “hook” the reader if the text hook fails or does not do a good enough job (the best visual hooks can stand on their own and are interesting images in their own right). 

The visual hook should illustrate, add supplementary information to, or provide an adjacent/alternative angle to the main point. Similarly to a text hook, a visual hook should embody/hint at/extend the main idea of the post. 

Examples of great pairings include but are not limited to: 
- The post is about an industry report, and the visual is a chart or screenshot from the report 
- The post is about the poster’s career progression, and the visual is a “before and after” comparison of the poster a long time ago vs. now
- The post is an ABM post tagging multiple people at a company, and the visual showcases all the people tagged. 

# AI writing
The following things are hallmarks of AI writing, which we do not want. AVOID using the following phrasing/language in post writing, or use them sparingly only when a post would really benefit from it:
- It's not just X; it's Y. (ex: "This isn't a feature update. It's a category shift.", "They're not the ones who ___. They _____.", "Not A. But B.")
- Casual hyperbolic statements (ex: "This one simple thing changes everything about how startups get built.")
- Overly-dramatic posturing about letting the reader in on a secret (ex: "And here's the quiet part no one says aloud..", "The signal behind the noise was...", "But here's the thing:..")
- The use of the word "quiet[ly]" to sound dramatic (e.g. "Here's the quiet part no one says aloud," "They lost access to an AI tool that had quietly become mission-critical.")
- Excessive use of em dashes
- Short dramatic lead-up questions (ex: "The real signal? Their merchant partnerships: ...", "The numbers that matter most? ...")
